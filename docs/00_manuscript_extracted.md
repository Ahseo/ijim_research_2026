Does AI Benefit All Platform Stakeholders? Evidence from Algorithmic Task Assignment in Food Delivery

Abstract

With the increasing adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) across industries, on-demand platforms have been at the forefront of implementing algorithmic task-assignment systems that match customer service requests with platform workers, thereby enhancing operational efficiency and workforce management. While such systems can streamline service matching and improve productivity, concerns remain about how equitably their benefits are distributed across key stakeholders, including platform workers and end-use customers. Using a unique context of a food delivery platform, we examine how algorithmic task assignment affects riders’ productivity, productivity disparities across riders, and customer service quality. Our results reveal that the adoption of task-assignment AI improves worker productivity by nearly 3% on average, with gains concentrated among medium-skilled workers (over 5%). The AI enables riders to complete more orders per stack (i.e., stack more orders) by assigning orders tailored to riders’ real-time conditions, thereby increasing daily orders and earnings for low- and medium-skilled riders. However, low-skilled riders work longer hours to complete extended stacks of orders, limiting their productivity gains, while high-skilled riders experience no apparent benefit. Overall, algorithmic task assignment partially alleviates existing productivity disparities. On the customer side, AI adoption shortens waiting times for single-order deliveries but does not improve waiting times for stacked deliveries, likely because riders engage in opportunistic behavior that prioritizes earnings over individual-customer waiting time. Taken together, the findings suggest that algorithmic task assignment does not uniformly benefit all stakeholders but can serve as a Pareto-improving operational strategy for on-demand operations. 

Keywords:  Task assignment algorithm, On-demand platforms, Labor productivity disparities, Service quality, Natural experiment design

1. Introduction

The on-demand platform economy—often broadly referred to as the gig economy—has grown explosively since the COVID-19 pandemic and has become an integral part of the global socio-economic landscape (Mckinsey, 2021). These platforms serve as intermediaries that connect customers demand with gig workers, offering customers more convenient, real-time services while providing workers with flexible labor opportunities (Duggan et al., 2023). With recent advances in machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI), algorithmic task assignment has become a fundamental component of on-demand platform operations, replacing human decision-making in matching customer requests with service providers (Kellogg et al., 2020; Möhlmann et al., 2021). Platforms like Uber and DoorDash employ these algorithms to efficiently assign ride and delivery requests to platform workers. Algorithmic task assignment is designed to manage myriads of workers in an optimized manner at scale, enhancing both overall system efficiency and workforce coordination (Duggan et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2015). The key advantage is its ability to improve the accuracy of the matches by taking into account the characteristics of customer requests and worker conditions (e.g., location, historical performance, estimated completion time). Given this advantage, many platforms argue that algorithmic task assignment not only improves system-level efficiency, but also creates value for key stakeholders—platform workers and end-use customers.

This claim of benefits for platform workers has been supported by recent studies on AI and labor documenting that, on average, algorithmic decision-making—beyond task-assignment algorithms—can improve individual worker performance and productivity (Bai et al., 2021; Dell’Acqua et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2021; Noy & Zhang, 2023; Sun et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2021). However, overall improvements in platform worker performance do not necessarily mean that all types of workers benefit equally. Performance gains may vary depending on workers’ levels of work proficiency. The varying impacts of algorithmic task assignment across platform workers raise a critical social question—whether AI serves to mitigate or worsen existing disparities and inequalities among them (Dell’Acqua et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Noy & Zhang, 2023). This question has gained prominence as AI is increasingly adopted in routine business operations.

Regarding the AI’s effects on inequality in worker’s performance, the findings in the literature are quite mixed. Some studies suggest that algorithms may exacerbate disparities in labor outcomes (e.g., productivity, earnings) due to biases in algorithm design and the nature of the historical data used for training algorithms (Y. Chen et al., 2024a; Chiacchio et al., 2018; Cowgill & Tucker, 2020; Korinek & Stiglitz, 2018). Other studies conclude that AI has the potential to reduce or at least not aggravate existing disparities in labor outcomes (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2018; Brynjolfsson et al., 2025; Cowgill, 2018; Dell’Acqua et al., 2023; Gries & Naudé, 2018; Gupta et al., 2022; Kleinberg et al., 2018; Noy & Zhang, 2023; Van de Gevel & Noussair, 2013; Webb, 2019). However, this literature seldom considers the issue of inequality among workers in the platform environment. As platform workers typically perform similar tasks and directly compete with one another, reducing inequality among them and raising the overall proficiency of workforce can benefit platform operations. Unlike employees in the traditional economy, platform workers face precarious income conditions, as their earnings depend entirely on performance—specifically, how many tasks they complete within a given period (M. K. Chen et al., 2019). Since their performance is strongly shaped by their job experience, new or lower-skilled workers often face a substantial learning curve before they become proficient. If task-assignment AI can shorten this learning curve by relieving new workers of task-selection burdens of selecting appropriate tasks, while alleviating skill disparities among platform workers without disadvantaging more experience workers, its adoption would be highly beneficial. Workers could adapt more quickly to their jobs and increase overall productivity. This, in turn, would help foster a fairer and more productive work environment while supporting platforms in addressing pressing challenges related to attracting and retaining workers.

Whether the advantages of task-assignment AI extend beyond improving average worker productivity to meaningfully alleviating existing productivity disparities among platform workers remains an open question. Task-assignment AI selects tasks for platform workers that best match their current circumstances, but this constitutes only the first step in a multi-stage workflow that requires different types of worker capabilities at subsequent stages. If worker’s ability to execute these later steps determines their productivity as much as, or even more than, task selection to overall productivity, the impact of task-assignment AI may be limited—particularly for those who lack the requisite skills. Alternatively, if task-assignment AI reallocates tasks from more experienced workers to less-skilled workers, it may reduce productivity gaps among workers but potentially at the cost of disadvantaging or disenfranchising experienced workers.

Another important issue surrounding the adoption of task-assignment AI is whether it can enhance customer service quality by reducing customers’ waiting times for task completion. Even if a new algorithmic approach improves system-level efficiency and benefits platform workers, it may not be a sustainable operational practice if these gains come at the expense of customer satisfaction. Therefore, it is essential to adopt a holistic perspective that considers both how algorithmic task assignment alters workers’ labor outcomes and how these changes ultimately affect customers.

Although prior studies on algorithms in online labor platforms have provided valuable insights, they have primarily focused on the impact of AI introduction on workers, while giving limited attention to customers as interrelated stakeholders (Jarrahi & Sutherland, 2019; Lee et al., 2015; M. Liu et al., 2021; Möhlmann et al., 2021). Because task-assignment AI can improve the quality of matching between tasks and workers and potentially enhance overall worker productivity, it may also positively influence customer service quality. However, there is also a possibility that the adoption of new technologies or policies may incentivize opportunistic behaviors among workers, as they become more focused on maximizing productivity and earnings—potentially at the expense of customer service quality (T. Liu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). This possibility highlights the need for empirical investigation into how task-assignment AI affects end-use customers.

We address this under-explored topic by empirically examining how algorithmic task assignment approach affects platform workers’ labor productivity, productivity disparities among them, and eventually, the quality of customer service in the context of a food delivery platform. Food delivery platforms present an ideal setting for this investigation. First, they have been at the forefront of task assignment algorithm adoption because making effective decisions on which orders to take is challenging for many food delivery workers (hereinafter referred to as “riders”), especially for beginners. When deciding to select a task, riders need to quickly take high-dimensional information into account, such as task characteristics (e.g., pick-up and drop-off locations, service fees, delivery time constraints) and their current situation (e.g., current location, in-progress orders). The strategic behavior of riders who simultaneously take on multiple orders (i.e., stacked orders) adds further complexity to their decision-making processes. Second, the completion of a food delivery consists of several task components (e.g., order assignment, order sequencing, routing, pickup, and driving). Since the assignment of an order is the very first and important component affecting subsequent task processes, the adoption of algorithmic order assignment can influence various aspects of riders’ delivery behavior, which in turn affect their productivity and the quality of their delivery service. Third, the impact of AI adoption in food delivery platforms can be heterogeneous across riders because individual skills in order-taking and completing subsequent task components vary significantly. These institutional characteristics enable us to investigate the heterogeneous effect of algorithmic task assignment on platform stakeholders and the resulting impact on inequality in labor productivity within the platform.

In this paper, we use novel order level data with worker identifiers from a food delivery platform in Korea, where the decision to take on a delivery order was initially left to individual riders. After years of operation, the platform introduced an AI-based order assignment system that automatically allocates each order to the most suitable rider. This institutional change provides a natural experiment that allows us to empirically identify the effects of introducing the new system on riders’ labor outcomes and customer service quality. Note that the platform did not mandate universal adoption of the AI system or require riders to accept every AI-assigned order, which could have resulted in selection bias associated with the riders’ adoption of the new system. To address this potential endogeneity issue, we carry out difference-in-differences (DID) and triple difference-in-differences (DDD) analysis using a propensity-score matched (PSM) sample.

Our results reveal that the introduction of the order assignment AI increases riders’ daily productivity—the number of orders completed per working hour—by approximately 3%. Notably, this productivity gain is concentrated among medium-skilled riders, whose productivity rises by slightly more than 5%, while no significant changes are observed for low- and high-skilled riders. The substantial improvement among medium-skilled riders appears to be driven by their ability to handle a greater number of orders within a single continuous trip (i.e., a stack). Importantly, this gain is achieved without increases in either the total number of stacked trips or their daily working hours. Alongside this productivity improvement, medium-skilled riders also experience increases in their total daily order counts and earnings. Low-skilled riders likewise complete more orders per stack following the AI introduction, leading to higher daily order counts and earnings. However, because theses riders need to work longer hours to process the extended sequences of orders, they do not realize improvements in overall daily productivity. High-skilled riders experience neither gains nor losses in productivity, suggesting that they were already operating near the upper bound of achievable daily productivity prior to the AI introduction. Thus, the productivity gains observed among other groups do not come at the expense of high-skilled riders’ performance. Taken together, these results suggest that the new algorithmic system helps narrow the productivity gap, particularly between high- and medium-skilled workers. 

Another key finding is that, while AI-driven improvements in worker productivity do not translate into shorter average customer waiting times, they do lead to shorter waiting times for single-order deliveries. In other words, most of the surplus generated by order-assignment AI accrues to platform workers through strategic behavior, particularly order stacking, whereas customer welfare gains are modest and confined mainly to single-order deliveries. In summary, although not all platform stakeholders benefit directly from the adoption of order assignment AI, no stakeholder is made worse off, suggesting that its adoption constitutes a Pareto-improving strategy.

Our study makes several important contributions to the literature on algorithmic approaches and business strategies in on-demand work platforms. First, to the best of our knowledge, this study provides  the first empirical evidence on how algorithmic task assignment simultaneously influences platform workers’ productivity and disparities among workers. By examining a comprehensive set of labor outcomes—including strategic order-stacking behavior, working hours, earnings—we show how task-assignment AI shapes worker productivity and why it may not fully eliminate pre-existing productivity gaps. These findings offer guidelines for platform managers by helping identify which groups of workers may require additional support and what specific work skills should be strengthened to enhance productivity and reduce disparities. Second, we present counterevidence to the commonly held claim made by on-demand platforms that AI-driven practices universally benefit all stakeholders, including customers. We demonstrate that the introduction of algorithmic decision-making does not necessarily improve customer outcomes—particularly in settings where workers engage in strategic behaviors that may be amplified by AI systems. Overall, this study deepens our understanding of the multifaceted impacts of algorithmic task assignment on both platform workers and customers, challenging potential misconceptions and unwarranted optimism surrounding algorithmic management.

2. Related Literature

Our study builds on the growing literature on the impact of algorithms that replace or automate human decisions on working conditions and workplace inequality. To date, much of the literature has examined how people perceive and trust algorithms, and how they are psychologically affected by AI (Castelo et al., 2019; Dietvorst et al., 2015, 2018; Dietvorst & Bharti, 2020; Dijkstra et al., 1998; Giuntella et al., 2025; R. Liu & Yin, 2024; Logg et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2019; Newman et al., 2020; G. Sun, 2023). Beyond individual perceptions or psychological aspects, some studies have examined how algorithmic decision-making affects labor performance at the firm-level (Damioli et al., 2021; R. Liu & Yin, 2024) and at the country-level (Frey & Osborne, 2013; Graetz & Michaels, 2018). 

Until recently, empirical research on AI’s impact at the individual worker level was limited, largely because researchers had limited access to high-quality, granular data from real-world workplace settings (Frank et al., 2019; Raj & Seamans, 2018). However, the rapid growth of AI and its integration into the day-to-day operations across nearly all industries and business functions have begun to alleviate this constraint. As a result, a growing—though still limited—body of studies have started to document empirical evidence on AI’s effects on individual-level work performance, including algorithmic task allocation and optimization in manual-work settings (Bai et al., 2021; Rad et al., 2025; J. Sun et al., 2022), AI-based assistance (Dell’Acqua et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2021; Zhang & Narayandas, 2025), and generative AI for cognitive work  (Brynjolfsson et al., 2025; Noy & Zhang, 2023). Yet, empirical evidence on how algorithmic decision-making influences the real-world output of platform or gig workers remains notably scarce, despite the fact that these businesses operate largely on algorithms and AI. Only a handful of recent studies have begun to explore this question—for instance, the effects of technology or algorithm-enabled tools on drivers in ride-hailing platforms (Y. Chen et al., 2024b; M. Liu et al., 2021), the AI-based guidance on batch-picking performance in grocery shopping platforms (Knight et al., 2024). While prior studies offer important early insights into AI in platform work, our study advances this literature in three key ways. First, we leverage large-scale, real-world data from a food delivery platform to causally examine the introduction of algorithmic assignment in a natural experiment setting. Second, beyond documenting heterogeneous productivity effects across workers with different skill levels, we unpack the underlying mechanisms by analyzing a rich set of labor inputs (e.g., working hours, idle time, total stacks and orders) and strategic worker behaviors (e.g., order-stacking decisions). Third, we further examine how workers’ decision-making interventions—such as engaging in single versus stacked deliveries—shape the downstream effects on consumer outcomes.

While algorithmic decision-making is often expected to benefit human workers, both academics and labor movement have expressed uncertainty about whether such systems can actually help reduce gaps among workers in labor outcomes, such as productivity, income, and employment. In this regard, the literature advances two competing views on the effects of algorithms on disparities in labor outcomes. One view argues that algorithms may reproduce or even amplify disparities due to biases in algorithm design or in the historical data used to train them (Y. Chen et al., 2024b; Chiacchio et al., 2018; Cowgill & Tucker, 2020; Korinek & Stiglitz, 2018). The other view contends that AI, which is fundamentally different from earlier technologies (e.g., information technologies, robots), has the potential to reduce or at least not exacerbate existing labor disparities (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2018; Cowgill, 2018; Gries & Naudé, 2018; Kleinberg et al., 2018; Van de Gevel & Noussair, 2013; Webb, 2019). Proponents reason that as AI has become more sophisticated, it can automate complex, non-routine cognitive tasks and in some domains outperform humans (Chui, 2017). 

We hypothesize that, on average, algorithmic task assignment can help enhance worker productivity and also alleviate productivity gaps among platform workers, with the effect being most pronounced for medium-skilled workers. For beginners, an order-assignment AI can improve order-rider fit by taking over the task of selecting appropriate orders given riders’ current situation, thereby increasing productivity and earnings. However, because the algorithm does not support the downstream execution tasks, beginners may still lack the skills and know-how needed to realize the full gains (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2018). Even if the assigned order fits well with their in-progress orders, they may struggle to bundle it into a stacked order, sequence pickups and drop-offs efficiently, or choose effective routes. Information overload can further limit their ability to translate algorithmic recommendations into performance improvements (Fiske & Taylor, 1991; Knight et al., 2024; Newell & Simon, 1972; Tversky & Kahneman, 1974). Thus, the productivity gains from algorithmic assignment may not fully accrue to low-skilled workers and may be limited for them. For highly experienced workers who already possess substantial know-how and make high-quality order-selection decisions, algorithmic recommendations offer little additional improvement. In addition, because these workers are confident in their abilities, they may rely less on AI suggestions than less-experienced workers (Chong et al., 2022), further reducing resulting in fewer opportunities for improvement. Accordingly, we posit that the marginal productivity gain from AI adoption is largest among medium-skilled workers—those constrained by neither too little nor too much experience—thereby narrowing productivity gaps primarily between high- and medium-skilled workers.

Our study also relates to the emerging empirical literature on the effects of algorithms on platform service quality and customer satisfaction in the context of on-demand platforms. On-demand platforms mediate interactions between a large set of workers (or service providers) and customers (or service requesters), employing a variety of algorithms to efficiently match them and to monitor and control operational processes (Berg et al., 2018; De Reuver et al., 2018; Möhlmann et al., 2021). Due to the nature of on-demand platforms, where both supply- and demand-side stakeholders participate and interact, the introduction of a new algorithm may affect not only platform workers’ performance but also customer service quality. So far, most studies in this domain have investigated the effects of AI introduction on platform worker’s perceptions and psychological well-being (Jarrahi & Sutherland, 2019; Lee et al., 2015; M. Liu et al., 2021; Möhlmann et al., 2021) and performance (Y. Chen et al., 2024b; Knight et al., 2024; M. Liu et al., 2021), with relatively little attention to how end-use customers may be affected. With the recent emergence of empirical studies based on real on-demand platform data, evidence in this area has only begun to accumulate. Overall, the effects on consumers have been mixed, varying by platform environment and the specific role of the algorithm (Y. Chen et al., 2024b; Knight et al., 2024; M. Liu et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2025). This suggests that the key question is not merely whether consumers benefit, but how algorithmic outputs translate into service outcomes—how theses algorithms reshape worker behavior along the way.

In the context of our research—the adoption of order-assignment AI in online food delivery platforms—we posit that it may not always benefit end-use customers. While worker gains from new technologies or policies can lead to improved customer service and satisfaction (Knight et al., 2024; M. Liu et al., 2021), this may not hold when platform workers engage in strategic behavior to maximize their earning opportunities and the introduced technology or policy further encourages such behavior, potentially undermining consumer utility (T. Liu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). For example, mobile app adoption by taxi drivers has been shown to increase hourly earnings through trip selections that favor longer rides rather than reduced cruising time (Wang et al., 2019). Thus, order-assignment AI may confer benefits to platform workers, but not necessarily in every delivery for customers.

3. Background and Data

3.1 Platform Overview 	

We collaborated with a food delivery platform in Korea. The platform takes delivery requests from multiple food-ordering platforms and provides on-demand food delivery services. Apart from the fact that the platform does not operate its own ordering channel through which end-use customers place orders, the remainder of its delivery operation is similar to that of typical food delivery platforms. The platform has more than 42,200 gig workers across over 500 regional management branches, serving 67,000 restaurants nationwide. The company allegedly uses one of the most advanced IT-based delivery services and mobile applications to manage their workers efficiently while meeting customer demand for prompt delivery. 

Like other food delivery platforms, such as Uber Eats and DoorDash, our delivery platform allows riders to complete either one order at a time or multiple orders simultaneously. The former is called single-order delivery, while the latter is stacked order delivery (see Figure 1). Here a “stack” refers to the set of order(s) that a rider handles within a continuous trip, not necessarily completed strictly one by one. Stacked orders arise when a rider accepts multiple orders from the same restaurant or takes subsequent orders while completing an existing order. Stacked-order delivery can benefit riders by reducing idle time and thus increasing earning opportunities. However, it has also been a source of customer dissatisfaction because these deliveries tend to take longer than single-order delivery. This is partly because riders may strategically optimize sequencing and routing within a stack to maximize their own earnings rather than to minimize waiting time for any individual customer.

Figure 1. Single-order delivery and Stacked order delivery 

The default order-assignment scheme on our delivery platform is manual dispatch. In this mode, the platform broadcasts a list of nearby customer orders—along with information on pick-up and drop-off locations, delivery distance, and service fees—and riders decide which order request to accept. When multiple riders compete for the same order, the first to accept it receives the assignment. After taking the order, the rider picks up the packed food from the restaurant and delivers it to the customers. 

Delivery operations under the manual dispatch scheme have several limitations. First, identifying an order that fits a rider’s situation is non-trivial. Riders must weigh multiple factors simultaneously, such as their current location vis-à-vis the order pick-up and drop-off locations, service fees, time constraints, and the possible delivery sequence and route across existing orders in the current stack and new candidate orders. Competition among riders further increases complexity. Because riders usually prefer orders with shorter distances and high service fees, they often make dispatch decision within a few seconds. When competition is fierce, less-experienced riders may miss lucrative orders or even orders well suited to their situation (i.e., location and in-progress orders), as they cannot process these factors quickly. To address this issue, platform operators have sought more effective ways to allocate customer orders to delivery riders with different skill levels, aiming to increase the platform’s operational efficiency, rider productivity, and customer satisfaction. This has recently motivated the introduction of algorithmic task-assignment systems. 

The delivery platform we collaborated with introduced their order-assignment AI in Busan, the second largest city in Korea, on October 26th, 2020. This AI system automatically assigns one order at a time to the rider it deems most suitable, based on both order-rider match quality and the rider’s revealed preferences. Match quality is determined using factors such as the rider’s location, in-progress orders in the rider’s stack, and the rider’s expected delivery routes. To capture rider preferences, the system uses each rider’s past delivery records to estimate a probabilistic distribution of order preference conditional on logistical situations. Importantly, the platform does not mandate their riders to use the order-assignment AI system. They can freely switch the AI mode on or off in the rider app. When the AI mode is turned on, however, the platform automatically assigns a best-matched order, and the rider should accept and complete the matched order. This AI assignment policy is intended to limit gaming the system, such as selectively accepting only the easiest or most profitable orders. 

3.2 Data and Variables

Our empirical analysis draws on a unique dataset containing delivery records of individual riders one month before and after the introduction of the order-assignment AI system. The dataset includes detailed order-level information, including anonymized rider identifiers, riders’ regional management branches, timestamps for each delivery step (i.e., order placement, assignment, pick-up, and drop-off), service fees, and the mode of order assignment (i.e., AI vs. manual). The data contain more than 800,000 records for orders from 900+ riders over the two-month period. Of these records, we retain approximately 600 riders who joined the platform before the observation window and have delivery records after AI introduction. Our primary variable of interest, daily productivity, is calculated as the number of delivered orders divided by the total working hours (i.e., the total amount of time spent delivering orders and idling in search of new orders). Daily productivity thus reflects a rider’s ability to complete more orders—including simultaneously via stacking—and to minimize idle time between stacks. The order-assignment AI system can increase daily productivity through two channels. First, the system assigns new orders based on a rider’s current location and, if any, information about in-progress orders and their likely delivery routes, enabling riders to serve nearby customers within a single trip without having to search for next orders. This advantage, however, generates productivity gains only when riders can efficiently manage sequencing and routing within a stack—completing more orders without substantially prolonging stack completion time. Second, by automatically assigning orders to suitable riders, the system can reduce idle time spent searching for next orders.

4. Empirical models 

Introducing order-assignment AI to the delivery platform provides a natural shock during our sampling period. The main empirical challenge in estimating the effects of AI is the potential endogeneity issue associated with the riders’ AI adoption decisions. For instance, riders who lag behind in competing for preferred orders may be more likely to switch to the AI system. If this is the case, adopters would constitute a selected sample, which could bias estimates of the effect of AI adoption. We address this potential endogeneity by performing difference-in-differences (DID) and difference-in-difference-in-differences (DDD) estimations (Meyer, 1995) on a propensity score-matched sample (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983).

We employed propensity score matching (PSM) to construct balanced pairs of treatment subjects (i.e., AI adopters) and control subjects (i.e., AI non-adopters). Using observations from the month immediately preceding the AI rollout, we identified a set of non-adopters that is statistically similar to the adopters. We estimated each rider’s propensity to adopt order-assignment AI using a logistic regression model (see Table A1 in Appendix). For the main analyses, we performed one-to-one nearest-neighbor matching without replacement under a caliper equal to 0.05 times the standard deviation of the propensity scores. Matching covariates capture riders’ delivery behaviors at the week, day, and stack levels. These covariates and their sample balances before and after matching are presented in Table 3. After matching, t-test results indicate that we cannot reject equality of means between the treated and control groups for any variables at the 1% level. The final matched sample consists of 336 riders.

To understand these differences, we examine AI-driven changes in stack-level labor outcome variables (see Table 5), including order-stacking behavior and idle time, and day-level outcomes (see Table 6), including daily earnings and daily working hours.

In the stack-level analysis, we find that medium-skilled riders who adopt the order-assignment AI spend less time per order within a stack, whereas no such effect is observed for low-skilled riders (see Column 3 in Table 5). This difference can be explained by two countervailing effects: AI adoption enables both low- and medium-skilled riders to operate longer stacks with more orders (see Column 1 in Table 5). Yet longer stack increases average stack completion time for low-skilled riders, with no corresponding change for medium-skilled riders (see column 2 in Table 5). 

Although the AI reduces the effort required to identify suitable orders given each rider’s situation and thereby makes it easier to stack orders, it does not guide riders on pickup and drop-off sequencing or routing within a stack. As a result, low-skilled riders—who may lack the know-how to manage sequence and routes efficiently—take longer to complete an extended stack. On the other hand, medium-skilled riders appear to convert improved matching into higher throughput, completing more orders within the same amount of time (or less time per order). In the case of high-skilled riders, the benefits from AI adoption are not apparent, likely because they have already optimized both order selection and execution, leaving limited room for further improvement.

In addition to enabling riders to carry out longer stacks, order-assignment AI may influence daily productivity by changing idle time between stacks. Because the AI system automatically assigns orders to suitable riders, it should shorten order search time and thereby reduce idle time. This conjecture holds true for medium-skilled riders (negative and significant at the 5% level) but not for high-skilled riders, perhaps because the latter already operate near peak efficiency (see Column 4 in Table 5). What runs contrary to our intuition, however, is that low-skilled riders exhibit an increase in idle time (significant at the 5% level). One possible explanation is that, after being assigned longer stacks, low-skilled riders need more idle periods to recover before taking new orders.

In the day-level analysis, the overall AI effects on labor outcomes are consistent with the stack-level results. Although AI adoption does not change the daily number of rider stacks (Column 1 in Table 6), it is associated with substantial increases in daily total orders and daily total earnings for both low- and medium-skilled groups (Columns 2 and 3 in Table 6). This pattern likely arises because order-assignment AI enables riders to complete stacks with a greater number of orders than before AI adoption, thereby increasing daily total orders and earnings. We find no statistically significant difference in the effects on daily total orders or earnings between low- and medium-skilled riders. The corresponding effects remain statistically insignificant for high-skilled riders, who likely have limited scope for further productivity improvement. 

When it comes to daily working hours, low-skilled riders end up working longer following AI adoption. This pattern is consistent with the stack-level results: for these riders, extended stacks combined with increased idle time translate into longer total working hours (Column 4 in Table 6). By contrast, for medium-skilled riders, whose daily productivity increases, working hours remain unchanged after AI adoption. This suggests that low-skilled riders may exhibit behavioral inertia (Allon et al., 2018), that is, increasing hours to earn more, whereas medium-skilled riders may follow a time-targeting pattern, maintaining roughly the same hours even if they can reach their usual earnings level sooner (Crawford & Meng, 2011; Farber, 2015).

Taken together, our results consistently show that the adoption of order-assignment AI helps narrow existing productivity (and also earnings) gaps among riders, primarily by bridging the gap between high- and medium-skilled riders. Yet, the AI system does not fully eliminate disparities in two respects. First, order-assignment AI alone is not sufficient to improve low-skilled riders’ labor productivity, although they still benefit in terms of daily total orders and daily earnings. Second, while the gaps in labor productivity and earnings between medium- and high-skilled workers shrink, they are not fully closed: these gaps decline by only about 20% compared to the pretreatment period (see Table 2).

5.2 AI impact on Service Quality

We extend our analysis beyond rider outcomes to examine potential spillovers to customers. Specifically, we estimate changes in customer waiting time as expressed by the average elapsed time between order placement and order completion. For this estimation, we slightly modify the specifications in Equations (1) and (2). The dependent variable becomes customer waiting time, and we control for the average restaurant-to-destination distance for orders in each stack. 

The results show that the estimated effect of AI adoption on customer waiting time is negative but not statistically significant (see Column 1 in Table 7). That is, the benefits of introducing the AI accrue primarily to service providers rather than to those receiving services. In this sense, AI-facilitated order-stacking is a double-edged sword. While order-stacking can raise rider productivity by enabling them to deliver more orders within a trip, it can also require longer completion time compared to single-order delivery. Consistent with this mechanism, our comparison of single-order and stacked deliveries (see Columns 2, 3, 5, and 6 in Table 7) shows that AI adoption shortens consumer waiting time when an order is delivered alone, but not when orders are stacked.

5.3 Robustness Checks

Although we address potential selection bias by combining the PSM approach with the DID (and DDD) estimation, residual observable and unobservable differences between AI adopters and non-adopters might still bias our results. To further validate the consistency of our main results, we conducted a series of robustness checks along multiple dimensions. First, we examined the parallel trend assumption for AI adopters versus non-adopters (and across the three rider skill groups) during the pretreatment period (see Tables A2, A3, A4, and A5 and Figure A1 in Appendix). We conducted a “placebo test” using only pretreatment data, assigning a placebo treatment date at the midpoint of our pre-adoption period. Across our main variables, we find no statistically significant placebo effects at the 5% level, indicating that the pretreatment trends are equivalent across rider groups. Second, we verified our results under alternative PSM specifications (e.g., matching with replacement, different caliper size, and alternative distance measures) and also report estimates without matching (see Table A6 in Appendix). Third, we performed a week-level analysis of our main variable, daily productivity, to examine week-to-week variation in the algorithm’s impact over the month before and after introduction. We find no significant effects in the pretreatment period, and the post-adoption pattern is broadly stable: AI adoption has no significant impact on low- and high-skilled riders, while the effect for medium-skilled riders remains statistically significant through the month except in the second week (see Figure A2 in Appendix). 

6. Discussion

Despite the increasing ubiquity of AI in workplace operations, empirical evidence on how algorithmic task assignment in on-demand platforms affects key stakeholders—platform workers and customers—remains limited. Using unique rider-order level delivery records from a food delivery platform in Korea, we examine how order-assignment AI affects riders’ daily productivity and productivity disparities across riders, and how AI-driven changes in rider behavior translate into customer service quality.

Our study yields several key findings. First, order-assignment AI improves riders’ average daily productivity, but these gains are driven primarily by medium-skilled riders. The AI system enables riders to complete more orders per day, often by facilitating stacking, which raises daily order counts and thus daily earnings for both low- and medium-skilled riders. However, low-skilled riders work longer to earn more with no improvement in daily productivity. For high-skilled riders, we detect neither meaningful gains nor losses from AI adoption. Second, these heterogeneous effects imply that AI modestly alleviates preexisting productivity gaps among riders. Third, the productivity gains do not translate into shorter customer waiting times, likely because riders engage in strategic behaviors to maximize earnings. Fourth, customer waiting times do decline for single-order deliveries—where such strategic sequencing is less relevant—after the AI adoption.

Our findings offer several key implications for operators of on-demand and gig economy platforms that are considering or have already implemented AI. First, while algorithmic task assignment may not fully bridge productivity gaps among workers, it can be an effective platform strategy for improving worker productivity and earnings. In particular, task-assignment AI appears to benefit workers with an intermediate level of task knowledge and know-how (i.e., medium-skilled workers), helping them become more proficient in their jobs. Although AI does not generate comparable productivity gains for less-skilled workers, it can still expand their earning opportunities by enabling them to take on more orders. This implication is especially important given that traditional firms often rely on structured on-the-job training programs that entail considerable time and resources. Such training, however, is difficult to implement in gig settings where workers are independent and relationships are often short-term. In the absence of formal training, a practical way to improve worker performance is to deploy algorithms that assist or replace human decision-making in selecting appropriate orders—a complex task that is particularly challenging for new workers. By lowering the cognitive burden of order selection, such AI-based strategies may reduce barriers to entry and help platforms retain their workers more effectively. 

A second implication is that additional support—algorithmic or non-algorithmic—seems necessary to further alleviate productivity gaps among workers. In the platform we study, the current algorithmic task-assignment system makes it easier for riders to carry out more orders simultaneously, but it provides little guidance on how to execute stacked deliveries efficiently. As a result, low-skilled riders who lack the capabilities needed to manage stacked orders do not experience productivity gains. Moreover, although medium-skilled riders are able to turn AI-enabled stacking opportunities into higher productivity, they still do not fully close the productivity (and also earnings) gap relative to high-skilled workers. In order to push productivity further and reduce inequality among riders, platforms may benefit from offering complementary algorithmic (or non-algorithmic) support for downstream task components following order assignment, such as recommending efficient pickup and drop-off sequences and delivery routes, routing that accounts for food preparation times in restaurants, and informing legal and safe parking spots at drop-off points. Such support could improve operational efficiency and help workers perform better and earn more, which in turn strengthen platforms’ ability to attract and retain workers. However, to the best of our knowledge, widely deployed tools that systematically assist these downstream task components remain limited and continue to evolve across food delivery platforms. More broadly, assessing the impact of introducing an AI algorithm should go beyond a dichotomous judgment of “good” versus “bad.” Instead, it requires careful attention to (i) the operational problems the algorithm seeks to solve and (ii) which skill components workers still lack.

A final implication is that platform operation policies that limit excessive strategic behavior among workers under algorithmic support may benefit both workers and customers. Algorithmic task assignment can increase throughput by enabling workers to complete more orders, but excessive order stacking may also cause work overload and eventual exhaustion, reducing efficiency gains and lengthening completion times for individual orders. One potential solution to this problem is to impose algorithmic constraints—such as a cap on the maximum number of orders that can be combined into a stack—which could improve service outcomes for customers, even including those in stacked deliveries, while also protecting riders from overload.

Despite our contributions, this study is not without limitations. First, since our data covers only one month before and after the launch of the order-assignment AI system, we capture only short-term effects of AI introduction. We acknowledge that the impact of algorithmic assignment can evolve over time. For example, learning with new technology often follows an s-shaped curve: the learning of workers new to the job is slow and effortful initially; intermediate-level workers improve rapidly as they acquire skills and accumulate know-how; and gains plateau for experienced workers as there is little left to learn. In light of this pattern, one-month experience with AI may not be sufficient for workers, especially low-skilled workers, to fully utilize the new technology and improve their job skills. It is therefore possible that the effects of AI would be larger in the longer term, and that subsequent spillovers to customers may also vary with time. Second, our empirical investigation focuses on a single task (delivery) on a single platform (a food delivery platform). Our setting is an on-demand or gig-economy platform where complexity in task-assignment decisions and skill disparities among workers are particularly prominent concerns for platform operators. It would therefore be valuable to examine whether our findings generalize to other similar (e.g., ride-hailing) or different platform settings (e.g., traditional employment, less labor-intensive, or less digitized contexts). Third, while our worker-level delivery records contain rich information—including order and rider geolocations and Euclidean distances between restaurants and delivery destinations—they do not include exact mileage or actual driving paths (e.g., GPS-based route data). Future work incorporating such data could help further uncover the mechanisms through which AI affects workers and customers.
