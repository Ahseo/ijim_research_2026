Associate Editor:
I invited two experienced faculty members in the relevant field to review the manuscript. While both reviewers acknowledge the novelty of the dataset, they have raised significant concerns that cannot be easily addressed. My own review aligns with their assessment, and I agree with their comments. I am not going to repeat their concerns, and my additional core theoretical and methodological concerns are as follows:

• Theoretical Framework: I have serious reservations regarding the study's theoretical setting, particularly the definition of "labour skill." Categorizing workers based solely on their performance prior to AI introduction is potentially misleading. A more accurate classification would be "high," "medium," and "low" performance.
• Research Focus: The study’s objectives appear unfocused. The measurement of "Customer experience" can be as well viewed from the labour perspective, failing to investigate the actual inter-relationship between customers and workers in the context of AI adoption into on-demand platforms.
• Social Impact Claims: I am not convinced the results address whether AI drives social equality. Without specific details on how the AI was implemented on this food delivery platform, the conclusions remain speculative. If the AI was not designed with social equality as a goal, it is unlikely to produce such an outcome organically.

Overall, the manuscript is in an immature state. As noted by both reviewers, the issues of residual bias and the insufficient data coverage duration (both before and after AI introduction) are critical flaws. The methodological issues regarding residual effects and data duration are structural and cannot be resolved within a standard revision timeframe. Significant further work is required before this research is ready for publication.

Reviewer #1: This manuscript examines the introduction of an AI-based task-assignment system in a Korean food-delivery platform and studies its impacts on (i) riders' productivity, (ii) productivity disparities across riders of different skill levels, and (iii) customer waiting times. The setting and dataset are rare and valuable, and the paper is generally well written and empirically careful. The core results, including average productivity gains concentrated among medium-skilled riders, limited gains for low-skilled riders due to execution constraints, and nuanced customer-side effects, are interesting and potentially publishable.
Strengths:
1) Data and context: Order-level delivery records with rider identifiers around a major operational change are exceptionally hard to obtain, and the dataset is a major asset.
2) Solid empirical execution: The PSM plus DID and DDD approach is thoughtfully implemented, and the paper reports a rich set of outcomes beyond a single productivity metric.
3) Mechanism-oriented operational interpretation: The focus on stacking, idle time, and working hours provides a plausible operational channel linking assignment to productivity differences across skill groups.

At the same time, I have several major concerns that should be addressed to strengthen the paper's positioning and to ensure that the causal claims, especially on the AI and consumer sides, are aligned with what the design can credibly identify.
Major concerns and recommendations
1) Contribution and novelty need sharper positioning
The manuscript currently reads as "AI improves matching and thus productivity," which is consistent with a broader literature on algorithmic dispatch and assignment across platform and operational settings. Given that a number of studies have already examined algorithmic assignment, including in ride-hailing and other platform work contexts, the paper would benefit from clarifying what is fundamentally new here beyond "algorithmic assignment matters."

2) Clarify what is "AI" (vs. conventional algorithmic dispatch) and why it matters
A central premise is that the platform introduced a "task-assignment AI." However, the manuscript does not sufficiently distinguish AI-specific components from standard automated dispatch and optimization that many platforms have long used. As a result, the contribution risks being interpreted as evidence on algorithmic assignment in general, not AI per se.

3) Remaining self-selection and endogeneity concerns in adoption
Although the rollout date is exogenous, the key treatment is riders' adoption of AI mode, which is not randomized. PSM plus DID and DDD helps, but residual bias may remain if adoption correlates with time-varying unobservables, such as rider learning and strategy changes, temporary income needs, fatigue, or demand and supply shocks at micro levels, that also affect productivity and order composition.
Suggestions to strengthen identification, and any combination would help:
A) Provide a more explicit event-study or dynamic DID around the rollout, or around adoption if feasible, for the main outcomes, with emphasis on pre-trend diagnostics.
B) Discuss and test sensitivity to alternative treatment definitions. The current "ever adopter" indicator can be too coarse if riders switch AI on and off or adopt at different intensities. Consider measuring AI usage intensity, such as the share of AI-assigned orders or days, and estimating dose-response patterns.
C) Consider robustness checks that address potential time-varying selection, such as alternative matching or reweighting, restricting to riders with stable working patterns, or using branch-level adoption patterns if relevant operational nudges exist.

4) Consumer-side inference is overstated
The paper positions consumer outcomes as a major contribution and suggests that findings challenge common beliefs about AI benefiting all stakeholders. However, the consumer-side estimation appears to be attached to the matched rider sample, so it measures waiting times for orders delivered by treated versus control riders. This is informative, but it does not necessarily identify platform-wide or market-level consumer welfare effects, because equilibrium and spillover effects could cause overall customer waiting time to move differently from the waiting time experienced by customers served by the matched riders.
Additionally, the evidence itself is nuanced. Average waiting time does not significantly improve, but single-order deliveries become faster. This supports order-type-contingent consumer gains rather than a broad conclusion that customers do not benefit.
If feasible, add platform-level aggregate analyses, such as branch-day average waiting time using all orders, trend plots, and regressions. Even descriptive evidence would help readers understand possible general equilibrium effects. Avoid strong welfare rhetoric unless supported. The appropriate conclusion seems closer to saying that consumer gains are limited and conditional on order type, and they may be attenuated by stacking and strategic sequencing.

Minor comments
1) Please check the reference list for potential duplication or labeling issues, for example multiple entries that appear to refer to the same working paper.
2) Consider reporting a more direct inequality metric, such as variance, Gini or Theil, or P90 to P10 productivity gaps, in addition to subgroup DDD coefficients, to make the inequality claim more transparent.
3) Clarify which fixed effects and time controls are used in the consumer waiting time regressions and whether they match the richness of the rider-side specifications.

Recommendation
I view this as a promising and largely solid paper with a rare dataset. Addressing the above points, especially sharper novelty positioning, clearer AI versus algorithmic dispatch characterization, stronger treatment definition and selection diagnostics, and more carefully scoped consumer-side claims, would substantially strengthen the manuscript and improve its chances of publication.


Reviewer #2: The given article discusses the impact of AI-based task-scheduling on a food delivery service platform on employee productivity and customer satisfaction. Artificial intelligence boosts average productivity by 3 percent, specifically between medium-skilled riders, although low-skilled workers have to endure more working hours, and high-skilled riders by a small margin. It is stated that, although the differences in productivity are partly diminished, there is only an improvement in the customer waiting times in single-order deliveries. In general, the AI advantages are not well distributed, yet they can increase the efficiency of operations.
I have read the papers, and my observations are as follows:
(1) The introduction section repeatedly suggests that AI "creates value" and improves productivity and efficiency. However, it is asserted before empirical evidence is introduced, and it glosses over potential negative consequences such as worker stress, algorithmic bias, or reduced autonomy.
(2) The paper studies both workers and customers; the introduction heavily emphasizes worker productivity. The discussion of customer outcomes is delayed and speculative, making the study's broader relevance to platforms less convincing. I suggest rechecking the information.
(3) The introduction describes the natural experiment and empirical methods but does not critically discuss limitations (e.g., selection bias, generalizability, cultural context, or applicability to other gig platforms). Also, I suggest establishing a clear research gap.
(4) Most of the literature review lists studies on psychological effects, firm-level performance, and individual-level impacts of AI, but it does not critically evaluate conflicting findings or gaps. The review mentions both positive and negative impacts on disparities, but it does not interrogate why prior results differ or under what conditions AI might help or harm different worker groups. Please check this section and update the information for the critical literature review section.
(5) The review acknowledges that evidence on platform or gig workers is "notably scarce," yet it largely draws on general studies of AI in workplaces, ride-hailing, or grocery platforms.
(6) The dataset covers only one month before and one month after AI introduction, which is very short for capturing stable behavioral changes or learning effects. Moreover, it has been mentioned that retaining 600 riders who joined before the observation window and have post-AI records, but it has not been discussed how representative these riders are of the broader platform workforce.
(7) Although the authors use PSM + DID/DDD, there remains concern that unobservable differences between adopters and non-adopters could bias estimates. For example, riders who self-select into AI might have higher motivation, tech-savviness, or familiarity with stacks that aren't captured by observable covariates. The robustness checks acknowledge this, but residual bias may still exist. In addition to this, my key concern is the analysis which is based on one month after AI introduction, which may not capture longer-term adaptation or learning effects, particularly for low-skilled riders. Productivity improvements observed in the short term may overestimate or underestimate sustained effects.
(8) The discussion also acknowledges that the data covers only one month before and after AI adoption, but it still heavily interprets the results as indicative of broader worker behavior and productivity trends.
(9) The learning curve argument is mentioned, but no empirical evidence supports it; this weakens claims about medium-skilled workers benefiting most and low-skilled workers not improving, because longer-term adaptation is unknown.
(10) The study focuses solely on one task (delivery) and one platform (Korea-based food delivery), limiting the external validity of the discussion's conclusions.
(11) There is no proper conclusion section in the paper, there are no theoretical and practical policy implications, nor limitations of the study.
