## 2.1 AI and individual worker performance

A growing empirical literature has begun to document how AI affects individual-level work performance. In manual-work settings, algorithmic task allocation and optimization tools have been shown to raise productivity (Bai et al., 2021; Rad et al., 2025; J. Sun et al., 2022). In knowledge work, AI-based assistance and generative AI raise individual output and quality (Brynjolfsson et al., 2025; Dell'Acqua et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2021; Noy & Zhang, 2023; Tong et al., 2021; Zhang & Narayandas, 2025). The magnitude and sign of these effects, however, vary substantially with task type, worker baseline proficiency, and the extent to which the AI substitutes for or complements human judgment. Dell'Acqua et al. (2023), for example, document a *jagged frontier*: AI raises performance on tasks inside its frontier but degrades performance when used beyond it.

Direct evidence on platform and gig workers remains comparatively scarce, even though these businesses are explicitly built on algorithmic decision-making (Frank et al., 2019; Raj & Seamans, 2018). A small number of recent studies have begun to address this gap: technology- or algorithm-enabled tools and ride-hailing driver outcomes (Y. Chen et al., 2024; M. Liu et al., 2021); AI-based guidance for batch picking in grocery delivery (Knight et al., 2024); and order-assignment and routing optimization in food delivery (Mao et al., 2025). **[R2-5]** These studies offer valuable early evidence, but they typically examine either worker productivity or system efficiency in isolation. Our study advances the literature by quantifying — within a single empirical setting — worker productivity, productivity disparities across pre-AI proficiency groups, and customer-side outcomes, and by making transparent the *boundary condition* (the multi-stage task structure) under which the heterogeneous gains arise.

## 2.2 Algorithmic decision-making and inequality in labor outcomes

Whether algorithmic decision-making widens or narrows labor-outcome disparities is an unresolved empirical question, and the literature offers two competing views. **[R2-4]** The first view holds that algorithms can replicate or amplify disparities, either through bias in algorithm design or through bias embedded in the historical training data (Y. Chen et al., 2024; Chiacchio et al., 2018; Cowgill & Tucker, 2020; Korinek & Stiglitz, 2018). The second view holds that AI — particularly more capable systems — can in principle reduce, or at least not exacerbate, existing labor disparities (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2018; Brynjolfsson et al., 2025; Cowgill, 2018; Gries & Naudé, 2018; Kleinberg et al., 2018; Noy & Zhang, 2023; Webb, 2019).

These two views are not mutually exclusive. Whether algorithmic assignment widens or narrows disparities depends on (i) which bottleneck the algorithm relieves, (ii) which workers were previously bound by that bottleneck, and (iii) whether other workers can translate algorithmic output into performance — that is, whether they possess the *complementary skills* for downstream execution. In our setting, the algorithm relieves the order-selection bottleneck, but downstream stacking, sequencing, and routing remain the worker's responsibility. The algorithm's distributional effect therefore turns on which proficiency group is most constrained at each stage. This framework helps reconcile the apparently conflicting findings in the literature and motivates the heterogeneous-effect specification we adopt.

## 2.3 Algorithmic decision-making and customer service quality on platforms

Beyond worker outcomes, algorithms in on-demand platforms also affect customer service quality. Because the platform mediates two-sided interaction, the introduction of a new algorithm can simultaneously reshape worker behavior and customer outcomes (Berg et al., 2018; De Reuver et al., 2018; Möhlmann et al., 2021). Empirical findings on customer-side outcomes are mixed. M. Liu et al. (2021) and Knight et al. (2024) find that algorithm-supported workers can improve customer service. In contrast, mobile-app adoption by taxi drivers has been shown to *increase* trip lengths through driver-favorable selection rather than consumer-friendly speed (Wang et al., 2019), and pricing-scheme reforms can interact with strategic driver behavior in non-obvious ways (T. Liu et al., 2019).

The decisive factor, again, is *worker behavior*. When new technology enables strategic responses (e.g., longer trips, denser stacks, selective acceptance), the customer-side outcome can move in the opposite direction from what worker-productivity gains alone would predict. Our paper directly examines this conditional dependence by comparing single-order versus stacked-order customer outcomes within the same matched sample.

## 2.4 Positioning of this study

This study contributes to the three preceding threads as follows. First, within the AI-and-platform-work literature, we provide the first joint empirical evidence on the distributional effects of order-assignment AI on platform workers and customers, with explicit attention to a boundary condition — multi-stage task structure — that we argue determines who benefits and who does not. Second, within the inequality literature, we operationalize the competing-views debate empirically, showing that the same algorithm can generate gains for medium-proficiency workers while leaving low- and high-proficiency workers materially unaffected on the productivity dimension.
