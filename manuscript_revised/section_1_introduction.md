The on-demand platform economy—commonly known as the gig economy—has expanded rapidly since the COVID-19 pandemic and now occupies a central place in the global socio-economic landscape (McKinsey, 2021). On-demand platforms intermediate between customer demand and gig labor, providing customers with convenient real-time services and offering workers flexible labor opportunities (Duggan et al., 2023). With recent advances in machine learning and artificial intelligence (AI), algorithmic task assignment has become a fundamental component of platform operations, replacing human decision-making in matching customer requests with service providers (Kellogg et al., 2020; Möhlmann et al., 2021). Platforms such as Uber and DoorDash use these systems to assign ride and delivery requests to workers at scale. Crucially, contemporary task-assignment systems are *AI-driven* in a specific sense: they incorporate revealed-preference learning from individual workers' historical choices and dynamic match-quality scoring updated in real time, distinguishing them from earlier rule-based or first-come, first-served dispatch (Kellogg et al., 2020; Mao et al., 2025; Möhlmann et al., 2021). **[R1-2]**

Whether AI-driven assignment delivers value broadly distributed across stakeholders, however, remains an open empirical question rather than an established fact. **[R2-1]** Although platform operators routinely emphasize that algorithmic assignment improves both worker matching and customer service, recent work in algorithmic management documents notable downsides — including increased monitoring and stress, opacity in assignment logic, and reduced worker autonomy (Jarrahi & Sutherland, 2019; Kellogg et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2015; Möhlmann et al., 2021). The empirical question we pursue is therefore *how* the benefits of algorithmic task assignment are distributed across stakeholders — workers of different pre-AI proficiency levels and end-use customers — once the assignment system is deployed in a real-world platform.

Two strands of related work motivate this question. First, a growing literature on AI and labor finds that algorithmic decision-making can, on average, improve individual worker performance (Bai et al., 2021; Brynjolfsson et al., 2025; Dell'Acqua et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2021; Noy & Zhang, 2023; Tong et al., 2021). Average gains, however, do not imply uniform gains, and the literature on inequality effects remains mixed (Y. Chen et al., 2024; Cowgill & Tucker, 2020; Korinek & Stiglitz, 2018; Noy & Zhang, 2023). Second, despite this rapid growth, direct evidence on platform and gig workers remains scarce (Knight et al., 2024; M. Liu et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2025), even though these are precisely the settings in which AI assignment systems are most heavily deployed. Evidence on consumer-side outcomes is even sparser, and the customer-side question is typically raised in passing rather than analyzed jointly with worker outcomes. **[R2-2]**

A more fundamental — and frequently overlooked — question is what features of the *task structure* in food delivery (and analogous gig contexts) make algorithmic assignment a non-trivial intervention. **[R1-1]** Food delivery is a *multi-stage* operation: once an order is assigned, it must be stacked with other in-progress orders, sequenced for pickup and drop-off, and routed across the city. Task-assignment AI optimizes only the first of these stages — the *match* between an incoming order and a candidate worker — and leaves downstream execution unchanged. The marginal benefit of algorithmic assignment therefore hinges on whether a worker can convert better matches into shorter completion time and a higher number of orders within a continuous trip. This boundary condition imposes a specific theoretical structure on the platform: AI assignment is most likely to benefit workers whose *bottleneck* is order selection rather than execution, and least likely to benefit workers who already excel at both stages. The same boundary condition shapes the customer-side spillover, because faster matches do not necessarily translate into faster individual deliveries when downstream stacking and sequencing remain at the worker's discretion.

We empirically address this question using novel order-level data with rider identifiers from a major Korean food delivery platform that historically left order acceptance to riders and introduced an AI-based order-assignment system in Busan on October 26, 2020. The system assigns one order at a time to the rider deemed most suitable, on the basis of a match-quality score that combines current location, in-progress orders within the rider's stack, and the rider's revealed-preference distribution learned from individual delivery history. We exploit the platform-level rollout as a natural experiment and address potential endogeneity in adoption — riders can opt in to AI mode — by combining propensity-score matching (PSM) with difference-in-differences (DID) and triple-difference (DDD) estimation across pre-AI proficiency groups.

We pose three research questions: (i) Does AI assignment increase average daily worker productivity, and if so, *for whom*? (ii) Does AI assignment narrow or widen productivity disparities across pre-AI proficiency groups? (iii) Does AI assignment translate into shorter customer waiting times for the customers served by adopting riders? We are careful to scope the customer-side estimand: our analysis identifies waiting-time changes for orders served by treated/matched riders, not platform-level consumer welfare effects, which would require equilibrium evidence beyond the matched sample (R1-4).

We find that order-assignment AI raises riders' average daily productivity by approximately 3% (DID coefficient = 0.141 \*\*\* on a baseline mean of 4.69 orders/hour). The gain is driven by medium-proficiency riders, whose daily productivity rises by approximately 5.4% (DDD = 0.249 \*\*\*), whereas neither low- nor high-proficiency riders show statistically significant gains. Beneath the average effect, AI assignment allows low- and medium-proficiency riders to operate longer stacks — more orders per continuous trip — with associated increases in daily orders and earnings for both groups. Low-proficiency riders, however, do not realize a productivity gain because their higher order counts coincide with longer working hours and greater between-stack idle time. Medium-proficiency riders, in contrast, complete more orders per stack in the same time, producing the observed productivity gain. High-proficiency riders show neither gains nor losses, consistent with their already operating near the operational frontier on both match quality and execution.

For customers served by treated/matched riders, the picture is more nuanced. Average customer waiting time does not change significantly; however, single-order deliveries become faster (DID = −0.184 \*\* minutes), while stacked-order deliveries show no improvement. This pattern is consistent with riders directing AI-enabled stacking gains toward their own throughput rather than toward customer-facing speed when they handle multiple orders simultaneously.

We make three contributions. First, to our knowledge, this study is the first to provide joint empirical evidence on how task-assignment AI in an on-demand platform shapes (i) average worker productivity, (ii) productivity disparities across pre-AI proficiency groups, and (iii) customer-side outcomes for orders served by treated riders. Second, we articulate and test a *boundary condition* for the value distribution of algorithmic assignment: gains accrue most to workers whose bottleneck is task selection rather than downstream execution, and accrue least to workers with limited downstream-execution capability. **[R1-1]** Third, we provide counterevidence to the often-implicit claim that algorithmic assignment uniformly improves customer service: in settings where workers can engage in strategic stacking and sequencing, customer-side gains are conditional and bounded.
